Change Detection in Arctic Lakes with the NASA Arctic and Boreal Vulnerability Experiment (ABoVE) Project

Team Members: Carley Fredrickson, Marta Wolfshorndl, Paige Lavin

Abstract

NASA Arctic and Boreal Vulnerability Experiment (ABoVE) project researchers want to study changes in individual Arctic lakes over the past 35 years. Field work is difficult in the Arctic so the researchers use a significant amount of satellite data. However, displaying tens of thousands of Arctic lakes on a view while also allowing for drill-down to individual lakes is tricky. Additionally, while Landsat satellites typically collect an image over an individual lake every 15 days heavy cloud cover obscures the lake's surface greater than 60% of the time in this region. This means that careful filtering must be applied in order to remove these cloudy scenes while not removing any especially bright (e.g., snowy) land in the process. The data files from Landsat are also quite large, requiring cloud computing resources such as Google Earth Engine (GEE) if to view them and significant scripting to remove clouds and examine a region of interest over time. In order to make these data more accessible and to facilitate quick comparisons between lakes, we developed a data visualization framework that allows our technical end-users to explore seven Arctic lakes of interest to our collaborator. This tool can easily be scaled up in the future to include any other lakes of interest to the broader, technical audience of the ABoVe science team.

Running Instructions

Check out the top three figures to get an idea of how the amount of Landsat data in this region varies interannually and seasonally over our time period of interest (1984-2019) and how the signals in the different satellite bands vary between lakes. To explore the Landsat data for a specific lake in more detail, click on its marker on the map and additional views will appear. First, a popup will open on the map giving you more detailed information, such as name, location, and other identifying features. Second, a timelapse video of your selected lake will load below the map. Third, timeseries charts for each satellite band's intensity over your selected lake will update. Please use Chrome for optimal interaction.

Many interesting processes can be seen in the videos such as:

  • the occurrence of wildfires as evidenced by scotched tan or red earth around a lake's edge (e.g, in 2005 at Canvasback Lake)
  • relatively low/high precipitation years, which show up as rapid loss/growth in the area of the lake (e.g., in 2007 and 2014 at Canvasback Lake)
  • algal blooms which turn the lake much especially green (e.g., in 1986 and 1999 at Ninemile Lake, 1989 and 2009 at YF17)
  • sediment input from rivers during rainy years (e.g., on the left side of YF20 in 2015)
